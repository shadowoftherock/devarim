## ‚úÖ 1. **Cohabitation/Live-in Partnership Agreement (Contract)**

Under Philippine law, unmarried couples (opposite-sex or same-sex) may enter into a **private contract** spelling out their rights and obligations as partners ‚Äî often called a *cohabitation agreement* or *domestic partnership declaration*. ([Respicio & Co.][2])

**What it can cover:**

* Division and ownership of shared property acquired during the relationship
* How expenses and finances are shared
* Agreements on living arrangements or responsibilities
* Terms for separation (if you choose one)
* Support obligations (where lawful)

**Legal basis:**

* The *Civil Code* allows parties to contract freely as long as the agreement:

  * Has mutual consent,
  * Deals with lawful objectives,
  * Has a valid cause,
  * Does not violate public policy or morals. ([Respicio & Co.][2])

**Important details:**

* **It doesn‚Äôt create a marriage-equivalent status.** Philippine law does *not* recognize ‚Äúcommon-law marriage‚Äù or domestic partnership as a legal family status the same way marriage is recognized. ([LawShun][1])
* A written and **notarized contract** is strongly recommended so it‚Äôs easier to enforce in court if disputes arise.
* You can also prepare a **separation agreement** now that sets terms for how things are handled if the relationship ends. ([Genie AI][3])

## ‚úÖ 2. **Declaration of Domestic Partnership**

This is a kind of *formal statement* that a couple is in a committed relationship. It doesn‚Äôt automatically create legal status, but it can help in practical situations:

* Company benefits (employer recognitions)
* Hospital visitation or designation of health decision-maker
* Support for immigration or documentation purposes (e.g., insurance, residency proofs)

Some cities (like Quezon City and San Juan) issue local cards/certificates recognizing domestic partnerships ‚Äî but at the **national level** these do **not** make you ‚Äúspouses‚Äù under the law. ([Wikipedia][4])

## ‚ùå 3. **It Is *Not* a Substitute for Marriage**

Because Philippine law sees **marriage as a unique legal institution**, any personal ‚Äúvow‚Äù or contract will not:

* Automatically create spousal rights (inheritance, spousal support under Family Code)
* Automatically recognize your relationship in legal contexts where marriage is required (e.g., certain tax benefits, spousal privileges, next-of-kin rights)

Only a marriage **under Philippine law** creates that status. ([RESPICIO & CO.][5])
